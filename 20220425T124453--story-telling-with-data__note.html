1. The importance of context

1.1. Exploratory vs explanatory analysis

  • Exploratory analysis is what you do to understand the data
    • To learn more chack Nathan Yau's book, Data points.
  • Explanatory analysis is what you do to explain the data

1.2. Who, what, and how

1.2.1. Who

1.2.1.1. Your audience:
  • you need to be specific on determining who is your audience and what they need to know or do.
1.2.1.2. You:
  • how your audience perceive you and what they expect from you.
  • do you need to work to establish your credibility or you are already trusted as an expert.
  • To learn more check Nancy Duarte's book, Resonate.

1.2.2. What

1.2.2.1. Action
  • What do you need your audiance to know or do
  • You should always want you audiance to know or do something, or else they wont care
  • There is always a false belive that the audience know better than the presenter
1.2.2.2. Mechanism
  • What amount of detail need to be shown
  • The amount of detail need to be base on the amount of control you have over the audience.
  • Live presentation: in this situation you have control over that the audience can see and adjust the amount of details base on audience questions or visual cues.
  • Written presentation: in this situation you don't have control over what the audience can see, thats why a decent amount of detail is needed.
1.2.2.3. Tone
  • What tone you use should be depending on what feeling you want your audience to have.

1.2.3. How

  • How can I get the data I need to help me make my point

1.3. Who, what, and how: example

  • Who: The budget committee that can approve funding for continuation of the summer learning program
  • What: Increase the budget of the summer learning program because it was succesful
  • How: collecting the student's before and after intrest in science using servey

1.4. Consulting for context: question to ask

  • often you don't have the context you need if the deliverable come from the request of a client
  • You can ask the followinf question:
    • What background information is relevant or essential?
    • Who is the audience or decision maker? What do we know about them?
    • What biases does our audience have that might make them supportive of or resistant to our message?
    • What data is available that would strenghthen our case? Is iuf audience familiar with this data, or is it new?
    • Where are the risks: what factors could weaken our case and do we need to proactively address them?
    • What would a successful outcome look like?
    • If you would only had a limited amount of time or a single sentence to tell your audience what they need to know, what would you say?

3. Clustter is your ennemie

  • cluster is anything that is not adding informative value
  • The more elements added to the graph, the harder it becomes for your audience to process it and get all the information.
  • Using the Gestalt principle, we can add information without introducing more elements to the graph.

3.1. Cognitive load

  • The more information added to the graph, the harder it becomes for your audience to process it.

3.2. Clutter

  • information that make it harder to understand

3.3. Gestalt principles of visual perception

  • Principles that were intreduce in early 1900s by Gestalt university to understand how individuals perceive order in the workd around them

3.3.1. Proximity

  • Objects that are close together are percieved as related or belonging to a group
  • to make focus on columns, increase the distance between columns and decrease it between rows.
  • to make focus on rows, increase the distance between rows and decrease it between columns.

3.3.2. Similarity

  • Objects that are of similar color, shape, size, or oriontation are percieved as related or belonging to a group.
  • this can be used in graph or tables to hint to the audiance where they need to look

3.3.3. Enclosure

  • Object enclosed together or inside a border are percieved as related or belonging to each other
  • one way to do it is to draw a shape, mostly rectangle, with a light background

3.3.4. Closure

  • Objects that are intermitent or discontinuous are precieved as a whole
  • The two axis in a graph almost give the illusion that that graph have a border, there for there is no need for another border

3.3.5. Continuity

  • Objects near to each other are precieved as smooth
  • as we saw with Closure, you can strip away some element and it will still precieved as a whole.
  • In case of bar charts, where there is a consistance amount of white space between bards, you can remove the axis and you can still see the axis

3.3.6. Connection

  • Connections comes as the second strongest assosiated value propriety, behind Enclosure
  • Connecting objects with lines makes them connect and establish their order to each other

3.4. Lack of visual order

3.4.1. Alignment

  • aligning text to the left instead of center make it easyer to read
  • diagonal orientation is also hard to read

3.4.2. White space

  • dont be afraid of leaving empty space in your report
  • don't try to fill empty space with more data

3.5. Non-stategic use of contrast

  • contrast is usefull to direct attention of your audience
  • don't over use contrast, it become harder for your audience to spot the important clues

3.6. Decluttering: step-by-step

  1. Remove chart border
  2. Remove gridlines
  3. Remove data markers (dots or shape that represent a data point)
  4. Clean up axix labes
    • remove zeros after the . and make number compact
    • shorten the word in axis if they can't fit horizontaly
  5. Label data directly (label the data at a point)
  6. Leverage consistent color (data and label are colored the same)

4.2. A brief lesson on memory

  • As we design visual communication keep in mind those 3 type of memorys

4.2.1. Iconic memory

  • super fast
  • it happend without the mind realize it
  • it give us the ability to quickly pick up diffirence in our envirment
  • related to survival mechanics
  • information stay in iconic memory for a fraction of a second before it get firwarded on to your short term memort

4.2.2. Short term memory

  • people can keep up 4 chunks of visual information in their short term meomry at a given time
  • creating a visual with alot of colors, data series, markers will be hard to grasp in the memory
  • we dont want to make the audience work to get the information
  • on selotion is to label data directly (reducing the work of gooing back in forth between the legend and the data

4.2.3. Long term memory

  • when something leaves short term memory, it is either lost or get passed to long term memory
  • long term memory is built up over time
  • Images can help us quickly recal things stored in long term memory
  • combining an image of a thing that hold deer or important to your audiance you trigger their long term memory and help them put in the new information in the long term memory as well

    • Story:

      • a story express how and why life changes
